## Ch. 7 — Cellular Respiration & Fermentation

### Core Equation
- Overall: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (ATP + heat)
- Aerobic respiration requires **oxygen**; fermentation does NOT use oxygen

### 3 Stages (in order)
| Stage | Location | Key Products |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| Glycolysis | Cytosol | 2 ATP (net) + 2 NADH + 2 pyruvate |
| Pyruvate oxidation + Citric acid cycle | Mitochondrial matrix | 2 ATP + 8 NADH + 2 FADH₂ |
| Oxidative phosphorylation (ETC + chemiosmosis) | Inner mitochondrial membrane | ~26–28 ATP |
| **Total** | | **~30 or 32 ATP per glucose** |

### Key Facts — Respiration
- Glycolysis = net **2 ATP** (substrate-level phosphorylation); occurs in **cytosol**
- DNA synthesis does NOT happen in glycolysis; glucose → 2 pyruvate
- Oxidation = loss of electrons; Reduction = gain of electrons (OIL RIG)
- Final electron acceptor in aerobic respiration = **O₂**
- Exact ATP yield is uncertain because phosphorylation is not directly coupled to redox reactions
- ATP yield also varies depending on whether electrons are carried by NAD⁺ or FAD
- Overall efficiency of cellular respiration ≈ **34%** (rest lost as heat)
- Glycolysis likely evolved early; occurs in cytosol (before mitochondria existed)

### Fermentation
- Fermentation = glycolysis + reactions that **regenerate NAD⁺** (allows glycolysis to continue)
- NAD⁺ regenerated by electron transfer from NADH to pyruvate or its derivatives
- Fermentation does **NOT** use an electron transport chain
- Anaerobic respiration **DOES** use ETC (but with different final electron acceptor, e.g., SO₄²⁻)
- **Alcohol fermentation**: pyruvate → acetaldehyde (releases CO₂) → ethanol; regenerates NAD⁺; used in brewing, winemaking, baking (yeast)
- **Lactic acid fermentation**: pyruvate → lactate directly; **NO CO₂** released; used for cheese, yogurt
- Human white muscle fibers can produce lactate even under aerobic conditions (fast but inefficient)
- Lactic acid fermentation by fungi/bacteria used to make cheese and yogurt

## Ch. 9 — The Cell Cycle

### Cell Cycle Phases
- Cell cycle = life of a cell from its formation to its own division
- Two major phases: **Interphase** (~90%) and **Mitotic (M) phase**
- Interphase = G₁ ("first gap") → **S** ("synthesis") → G₂ ("second gap")
- Chromosomes are duplicated **only during S phase**
- G₁ = cell grows; G₂ = cell prepares for division; S = DNA replication

### Mitosis Phases (in order)
1. **Prophase** — chromosomes condense; spindle begins forming
2. **Prometaphase** — nuclear envelope breaks down; kinetochore microtubules attach
3. **Metaphase** — centromeres align at metaphase plate (midpoint between poles)
4. **Anaphase** — sister chromatids separate; move to opposite poles
5. **Telophase** — nuclear envelopes reform; chromosomes decondense

### Mitotic Spindle
- Mitotic spindle = microtubules + associated proteins; controls chromosome movement
- In animal cells, spindle microtubules assemble from the **centrosome** (microtubule organizing center)
- Centrosome duplicates during **interphase**; two centrosomes migrate to opposite poles during prophase/prometaphase
- Aster = radial array of short microtubules extending from each centrosome
- **Kinetochores** = protein complexes at centromeres; attach to kinetochore microtubules during prometaphase
- Metaphase plate = imaginary structure at midway point between spindle's two poles
- In anaphase: sister chromatids separate; microtubules shorten by depolymerizing at kinetochore ends
- Nonkinetochore microtubules from opposite poles overlap and push against each other, elongating the cell

### Cytokinesis
- **Animal cells**: cytokinesis by **cleavage** → forms cleavage furrow (contractile ring of microfilaments)
- **Plant cells**: **cell plate** forms during cytokinesis (NOT cleavage furrow)
- Cytokinesis begins during anaphase or telophase

### Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes
- Prokaryotes reproduce by **binary fission** (not mitosis)
- Binary fission: chromosome replicates from **origin of replication**; plasma membrane pinches inward
- Mitosis probably evolved from binary fission
- Human somatic cells (nonreproductive) = **46 chromosomes** (2 sets of 23)
- Human gametes (sperm, eggs) = **23 chromosomes** (half of somatic)
- Eukaryotic chromosomes = **chromatin** (DNA + protein)

### Cell Cycle Control & Cancer
- Cell cycle controlled by **checkpoints**: G₁, G₂, and M checkpoints
- **G₁ checkpoint** = most important; called restriction point in mammalian cells
- If no go-ahead signal at G₁ → cell exits to **G₀** (nondividing state)
- If go-ahead signal at G₁ → cell usually completes S, G₂, M, and divides
- Cancer cells do NOT respond to signals that normally regulate cell cycle
- Cancer cells do NOT need growth factors to grow and divide
- Cells that divide indefinitely in culture have undergone **transformation**
- **Benign tumor**: stays at original site
- **Malignant tumor**: invades surrounding tissues; undergoes **metastasis** (spreads to other body parts)
- Localized tumors: treated with radiation; metastatic tumors: treated with **chemotherapy**
- Chemotherapy side effects because drugs also affect normal cells that divide frequently

## Ch. 19 — Evolution: Descent with Modification

### Darwin & History
- Darwin published *On the Origin of Species* in **1859**
- Darwin's voyage: HMS Beagle, **December 1831 – October 1836**
- Alfred Russel Wallace independently developed similar theory; jointly presented **July 1858**
- Galápagos Islands finch beak variation = evidence Darwin observed
- Evolution = "**descent with modification**"
- "Descent" = shared ancestry; "Modification" = accumulation of differences over generations
- **Georges Cuvier** developed paleontology; fossils found in sedimentary rock strata (younger = top)
- **Lamarck** proposed use/disuse + inheritance of acquired characteristics — **NOT supported**

### Natural Selection
- Natural selection: individuals with favorable **heritable** traits survive and reproduce more
- **Populations** (NOT individuals) evolve
- Natural selection only acts on **heritable** traits that differ among individuals
- Natural selection does **NOT create** new traits — selects among existing heritable variation
- Traits that are adaptive vary by place and time
- Evolution occurs more rapidly in species with short generation times
- **Artificial selection**: selective breeding for desired traits (e.g., wild mustard → broccoli, cabbage, kale)

### 4 Types of Evidence for Evolution
1. **Direct observations** (e.g., MRSA antibiotic resistance)
2. **Homology** (similarity from common ancestry)
3. **Fossil record**
4. **Biogeography**

### Key Cases
- MRSA: *S. aureus* became resistant to penicillin within **2 years** of widespread use (by 1945); then methicillin resistance also evolved within 2 years
- Fossil transition (whale evolution): Pakicetus → Rodhocetus → Dorudon → living cetaceans
- Homology ≠ Analogy: **Homology** = from common ancestor; **Analogy** = convergent evolution
- Convergent evolution (analogous features): sugar gliders vs. flying squirrels — NOT evidence of common ancestry
- **Biogeography** = scientific study of geographic distribution of species

## Ch. 20 — Phylogeny and the Tree of Life

### Classification
- **Phylogeny** = evolutionary history of a species or group
- **Systematics** = classifies organisms AND determines evolutionary relationships
- **Taxonomy** = ordered division and naming of organisms
- **Binomial nomenclature**: introduced by Carolus Linnaeus (18th century)
- Format: Genus (capitalized) + specific epithet (lowercase); entire name **italicized**
- Both parts together name the species (NOT just the specific epithet alone)

### Taxonomic Hierarchy (LEAST → MOST inclusive)
**S**pecies → **G**enus → **F**amily → **O**rder → **C**lass → **P**hylum → **K**ingdom → **D**omain

Mnemonic: **S**tupid **G**irls **F**ight **O**ver **C**hocolate **P**udding **K**illed **D**ad

- Example: Leopard = *Panthera pardus*; Family Felidae; Order Carnivora; Class Mammalia; Phylum Chordata; Kingdom Animalia; Domain Eukarya
- **Taxon** (pl. **taxa**) = named taxonomic unit at any level
- 3 Domains: **Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya**

### Phylogenetic Trees
- Phylogenetic trees = branching diagrams representing evolutionary hypotheses
- **Branch point** = divergence of two lineages from a common ancestor
- **Sister taxa** = groups sharing an immediate common ancestor
- Branches CAN be rotated without changing depicted relationships
- Trees can be drawn horizontally, vertically, or diagonally — relationships unchanged
- Unlabeled internal nodes/branches can represent extinct groups
- Only **homologies** (NOT analogies) can be used to infer phylogenetic relationships
- Analogy = similarity from convergent evolution → does NOT indicate common ancestry

## Ch. 41 — Ecological Communities

### Interspecific Interactions
| Interaction | Effect | Example |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Competition | −/− | Two species competing for same food |
| Predation | +/− | Lion eats zebra |
| Herbivory | +/− | Caterpillar eats leaves |
| Parasitism | +/− | Tapeworm in host |
| Mutualism | +/+ | Acacia trees + ants |
| Commensalism | +/0 | Cattle egrets on buffalo |

### Competition
- Competitive exclusion principle: two species competing for the **same limiting resource** cannot permanently coexist
- Classic example: *Paramecium aurelia* drives *P. caudatum* to extinction when grown together
- **Ecological niche** = specific set of biotic and abiotic resources used by an organism
- **Resource partitioning** = niche differentiation that allows coexistence
- **Character displacement** = differences in characters are greater when species are **sympatric** (same range) than **allopatric** (different ranges)

### Predation Defenses
- Cryptic coloration = camouflage (blends with background)
- **Aposematic coloration** = bright warning colors (signals toxicity)
- **Batesian mimicry** = harmless species mimics a harmful species in appearance

### Parasitism
- **Endoparasites**: live inside the host
- **Ectoparasites**: live outside the host

### Species Diversity & Community Structure
- **Species diversity** = species richness + relative abundance
- **Species richness** = number of different species in community
- **Relative abundance** = proportion each species represents of all individuals
- Shannon diversity index: H = −(p_A ln p_A + p_B ln p_B + ...)
- **Trophic structure**: feeding relationships; food chains link from producers to top carnivores
- **Food web** = interconnected food chains with complex trophic interactions
- **Foundation species**: strong community effects due to **large size or high abundance**
- **Keystone species**: strong community control via **pivotal ecological role** (NOT necessarily abundant)
- **Ecosystem engineers**: cause physical changes in the environment (e.g., beavers build dams)
- **Bottom-up model**: nutrient supply/lower trophic levels limit abundance at higher levels
- **Top-down model**: predation controls community organization (predators limit herbivores which limit producers)
- **Biomanipulation** = application of top-down model to improve water quality in polluted lakes

### Disturbance & Succession
- **Disturbance** = event that changes community by removing organisms or altering resource availability
- **Intermediate disturbance hypothesis**: moderate disturbance promotes **greatest** species diversity
- High disturbance → excludes slow-growing species; Low disturbance → competitive dominants exclude others
- **Ecological succession** = sequential change in community composition after major disturbance
- **Primary succession**: starts in nearly **lifeless** area (volcanic island, glacier retreat)
- **Secondary succession**: starts where disturbance removed most but NOT all organisms (e.g., after fire)
- Human activities = **strongest** disturbances to ecosystems; typically **reduce** species diversity

## Ch. 42 — Ecosystems and Energy

### Ecosystem Fundamentals
- Ecosystem = all organisms in a community + **abiotic factors** they interact with
- Two key emergent properties: **energy flow** + **chemical cycling**
- Energy **FLOWS** through ecosystems (not recycled); chemicals **CYCLE** within ecosystems
- 1st law of thermodynamics: energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transferred/transformed
- 2nd law of thermodynamics: every energy exchange increases entropy; some energy always lost as **heat**
- Law of conservation of mass: matter cannot be created/destroyed; chemical elements **CAN** be recycled

### Primary Production
- ~**1%** of visible light striking photosynthetic organisms is converted to chemical energy
- **GPP** (Gross Primary Production) = total primary production
- **NPP** (Net Primary Production) = GPP − autotrophic respiration = new biomass added
- **NEP** (Net Ecosystem Production) = total biomass accumulation rate
- NEP > 0 → carbon **sink** (gains more carbon than it loses)
- NEP < 0 → carbon **source** (loses more carbon than it gains)
- Climate change can cause ecosystems to switch from carbon sinks to carbon sources

### Trophic Levels & Energy Transfer
| Level | Type | Examples |
|-------|------|---------|
| Primary producers | Autotrophs (make own food) | Plants, phytoplankton |
| Primary consumers | Herbivores | Zooplankton, insects |
| Secondary consumers | Carnivores | Small fish, mice |
| Tertiary consumers | Carnivores | Hawks, large fish |
| Detritivores / Decomposers | Feed on detritus | Prokaryotes, fungi |

- **Trophic efficiency** ≈ **10%** of production transferred from one trophic level to the next
- Example pyramid: 1,000,000 J sunlight → 10,000 J producers → 1,000 J primary consumers → 100 J secondary → 10 J tertiary
- **Secondary production** = chemical energy in food converted to new biomass
- **Production efficiency** = net secondary production / assimilation of primary production × 100%
- Assimilation = total energy consumed and used in growth, reproduction, AND respiration

### Limiting Nutrients
- **Limiting nutrient** = element that, when added, increases production
- **Marine**: nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) most often limit production; **iron (Fe)** also limiting in some areas (e.g., Sargasso Sea — adding N + P + Fe increased production 12×)
- **Terrestrial**: nitrogen limits plant growth most **globally**; phosphorus can also limit (especially older soils)
- Adding a limiting nutrient only increases production until some other nutrient becomes limiting
- Light penetration in aquatic: half of solar radiation absorbed in **first 15 m**; only 5–10% reaches **75 m**
- **Eutrophication** = dramatic increase in primary production from excess nutrients in aquatic ecosystems

### Nutrient Cycling & Decomposition
- Nutrient cycles = **biogeochemical cycles** (involve both biotic and abiotic components)
- Biogeochemical cycles may be global or local
- Decomposer growth controlled by temperature, moisture, and nutrient availability
- Decomposition rates **faster in warmer** ecosystems
- Hubbard Brook Experimental Forest: nutrient losses much greater in **deforested** site vs. intact forest
- Plants control amount of nutrients leaving intact forest ecosystems
- **Bioremediation** = use of organisms (prokaryotes, fungi, plants) to detoxify polluted ecosystems
- **Biological augmentation** = using organisms to **add** essential materials to degraded ecosystems (e.g., nitrogen-fixing lupins to add N; mycorrhizal symbionts to improve nutrient access)

## High-Risk True/False Traps

| Common Trap | Correct Fact |
|------------|--------------|
| "Individuals evolve" | POPULATIONS evolve, not individuals |
| "Natural selection creates new traits" | It selects EXISTING heritable variation |
| "Fermentation uses ETC" | Only anaerobic RESPIRATION uses ETC; fermentation does NOT |
| "Lactic acid fermentation releases CO₂" | Alcohol fermentation releases CO₂; lactic acid does NOT |
| "ATP yield from respiration is exactly 32" | It's APPROXIMATELY 30 or 32 (uncertain) |
| "Chromosomes duplicate in G₁" | Chromosomes duplicate ONLY in S phase |
| "Cleavage furrow forms in plant cells" | Cleavage furrow = ANIMAL cells; cell plate = PLANT cells |
| "Analogous features indicate common ancestry" | Only HOMOLOGOUS features indicate common ancestry |
| "Specific epithet alone names a species" | BOTH genus + specific epithet together name the species |
| "Energy cycles within ecosystems" | Energy FLOWS through; chemicals CYCLE |
| "10% efficiency means 90% is wasted" | TRUE — only ~10% transferred to next trophic level |
| "Keystone species are highly abundant" | Keystone species control by ROLE, not abundance |
| "Primary succession follows fire" | Fire = secondary succession; primary = lifeless areas |
| "G₂ checkpoint is most important" | G₁ checkpoint is most important (restriction point) |
| "Benign tumors undergo metastasis" | Only MALIGNANT tumors metastasize |
| "Lamarck's theory is accepted" | Lamarck's use/disuse inheritance is NOT supported |
| "Domain is LESS inclusive than Kingdom" | Domain is MORE inclusive than Kingdom |
